A place for every hub, and every hub in its place.

The tab-row runs out of room past a few hubs and tells you nothing about which world you're in. why this scales where the tab-row doesn't

Four moves — each turning "how many hubs fit" into "no ceiling."

▚

Grouped by trust, always

Hubs live under Home / Shared / Foreign headers, colored by tier. You never scan a flat list wondering whose hub is whose — the structure carries the trust boundary the review said we were flattening.

law #2 · trust domains stay separate
⌖

A stable home you reach by memory

Each hub keeps its spot in the rail. After a day you reach for orbit without reading — muscle memory, not a hunt. That's the Astrolabos law working on navigation: position encodes identity.

Astrolabos · position = identity
◐

The workspace tells you which world you're in

The active hub's tier tints the whole workspace — a magenta edge + "foreign hub" pill on someone else's turf, teal on your own. The orientation is ambient and constant, not a label you have to go read.

P1 · "keep me oriented"
⌘

No ceiling: scroll, or ⌘K

Twenty hubs? The rail scrolls, grouped; ⌘K jumps you anywhere in two keystrokes, filtered and tier-labeled. The horizontal tab-row simply runs out of pixels — this doesn't.

P1 · won't-scale, solved

The same fleet, two ways to move through it

today · the tab-row
  • Horizontal tabs run out of room past ~4 hubs
  • Internal and foreign hubs sit side by side, undifferentiated
  • Nothing tells you which hub's world you're currently in
  • No fast jump — you hunt along the row
this redesign · the trust-tiered rail
  • Vertical, grouped, scrollable — no hub ceiling
  • Home / Shared / Foreign, tier-colored, never confused
  • Workspace tinted by the active hub's tier — always oriented
  • ⌘K jumps anywhere, filtered + tier-labeled

Only codeshrew/confer-lab (shared) and codeshrew/confer-jarvis-orbit (foreign) are live today; the others illustrate how the rail holds together as you federate with more collaborators. Health dots + tiers are real signals — a `null` would read unknown (grey), never a fake green.
